Abstract 

Large Language Models (LLMs) have demon-strated strong capabilities in natural language understanding and reasoning, while recent ex-tensions that incorporate visual inputs enable them to process multimodal information. De-spite these advances, Large Vision-Language Models (LVLMs) incur substantial computa-tional and memory costs, hindering deployment in resource-constrained scenarios. Existing layer pruning methods typically rely on local similarity metrics or static proxy signals, fail-ing to capture the global and dynamic evolution of representations across model depth, which often leads to the removal of transition-critical layers. To address this limitation, we propose a topology-aware layer pruning framework for LVLMs. Specifically, we represent layer wise hidden states as point clouds and models their evolution using simplicial complexes . By lever-aging zigzag persistent homology , we quantify inter-layer topological consistency and enable adaptive pruning that preserves critical repre-sentational transitions. Extensive experiments on diverse multimodal benchmarks demon-strate that the proposed framework consistently outperforms existing pruning methods across a wide range of sparsity ratios. Our code is avail-able at https://github.com/zpc456/TopoVLM.

To reduce computational overhead, recent works have explored layer pruning as an effective model compression strategy (Ma et al., 2023; Chen et al., 2024c). Existing methods generally fall into two paradigms: similarity-based and signal-driven pruning. Similarity-based approaches, e.g., 

LLM-Streamline (Chen et al., 2024c) and LLM-Pruner (Ma et al., 2023), are originally proposed for unimodal LLMs and later extended to multimodal

VL settings (Zhu et al., 2023; Li et al., 2024a). As illustrated in Figure 1, these methods remove layers based on predefined similarity measures ( e.g., co-sine similarity). In contrast, signal-driven methods, including SparseGPT (Frantar and Alistarh, 2023), Wanda (Sun et al., 2023), and ECoFLaP (Sung et al., 2023), rely on proxy signals such as weight magnitude, activation statistics, or attention maps. Despite their success, the black-box nature of these methods raises significant concerns regard-ing interpretability and transparency. They offer only a static view of internal representations and are limited in their ability to track representational changes across layers. Such local snapshot crite-ria are ill-suited to LVLMs, whose representations evolve in a structured manner along depth, transi-tioning from fine-grained visual encoding to VL alignment and instruction-conditioned reasoning. 

As a result, layers that appear locally redundant may in fact act as critical bridges between distinct semantic stages. Pruning these layers can disrupt global representational continuity and lead to non-linear performance degradation. This observation naturally motivates the following question: can we design a pruning criterion that captures represen-tation evolution as a global and dynamic process? 

A natural framework for addressing this ques-tion is Topological Data Analysis (TDA), which characterizes the global geometric and structural organization of high-dimensional data beyond lo-cal pairwise statistics. By focusing on topologi-cal invariants, e.g., connectivity, loops, and higher-order structures that persist across scales, TDA en-ables the analysis of representation dynamics as coherent systems rather than isolated activations. This perspective has recently attracted growing in-terest in machine learning (Ballester et al., 2023; Pham et al., 2025; dos Santos et al., 2025). Among TDA tools, persistent homology plays a central role by tracking the birth and death of topological features across scales, and has been used to study the stability of learned representations under dis-tribution shifts and other perturbations (Corradini et al., 2025; Brito-Pacheco et al., 2025; Athreya and Rosen, 2025). Despite these advances, translating topologi-cal insights into actionable criteria for structured model compression remains underexplored. This gap motivates an intuitive question: can persis-tent homology be leveraged to design a more ef-fective layer pruning method for LVLMs, where representations undergo non-monotonic transfor-mations driven by multimodal fusion and instruc-tion conditioning? Addressing this challenge re-quires a framework that tracks representation evo-lution across layers while accounting for both con-tinuity and structural overlap. To this end, we model the internal representations of LVLMs as a sequence of point clouds evolving along the model depth and interpret this evolution as a discrete dy-namic system. As illustrated in Figure 1(c), we ex-ploit zigzag persistence to characterize time (layer)-varying point clouds and temporal structures. The proposed topology-aware layer pruning framework tracks how topological features, e.g., loops, voids, and higher order structures emerge, persist, and disappear across the entire depth of the language decoder. With the proposed inter-layer topological consistency, we identify layers whose representa-tional roles are structurally redundant within the global model topology. This formulation enables pruning decisions that are guided by the global and dynamic evolution of representations across layers rather than local and static snapshots derived from individual layers or isolated proxy signals. To conclude, the main contributions of this paper are as follows: 

• To the best of our knowledge, we present the first topology-aware layer pruning frame-work tailored for LVLMs. By constructing 

zigzag filtrations over layer-wise represen-tations with k-nearest-neighbor graphs, the proposed method captures the dynamic struc-tural evolution of multimodal representations across Transformer layers. 

• We introduce inter-layer topological consis-tency as a principled measure of structural redundancy between layers and develop a topology-adaptive pruning strategy that pre-serves critical representational transitions. 

• Extensive experiments on standard VL bench-marks validate the effectiveness of the pro-posed framework. Results on LLaVA-NeXT and VideoLLaMA2 demonstrate consistent improvements over existing methods, even un-der high pruning ratios.

2.2 Topological Data Analysis 

The integration of TDA into deep learning has pro-gressed from external regularization to architecture-aware modeling. Early works primarily employ TDA as a regularization mechanism. For exam-ple, TopoSeg (He et al., 2023) introduces topology-aware loss functions to enforce global structural connectivity in semantic segmentation. More recent approaches embed topological priors di-rectly into model design, as exemplified by HG-Former (Wang et al., 2025), which leverages hy-pergraph learning to capture high-order semantic correlations beyond pairwise attention. In LLMs, TDA has emerged as a principled framework for characterizing internal representa-tions and guiding efficiency-oriented optimization. Recent studies apply high-order topological met-rics to analyze reasoning dynamics. Bazarova et al. (Bazarova et al., 2025) propose Manifold Topol-ogy Divergence to detect hallucinations by identify-ing structural breaks in attention graphs, while Tan et al. (Tan et al., 2025) use topological signatures to distinguish genuine chain-of-thought reasoning from memorization.

3.1 Preliminary Knowledge 

TDA provides a principled framework to character-ize the geometric and structural properties of com-plex datasets. A core tool in TDA is persistent ho-mology, which tracks how topological features of a point cloud evolve along a filtration, i.e., a sequence of simplicial complexes K1 ⊆ K2 ⊆ · · · ⊆ KT .Each inclusion induces a linear map between ho-mology groups, allowing features to be represented by their birth and death indices, which together form a persistence diagram. Classical persistent homology assumes a mono-tonic filtration. Zigzag persistence generalizes this by permitting both forward and backward inclu-sions. Formally, a zigzag filtration is a sequence, 

i.e., χ : K1 ←→ K2 ←→ · · · ←→ Kn, where each arrow denotes either a forward map Ki → Ki+1 

or a backward map Ki ← Ki+1 . Applying the p-th homology functor with coefficients in a field k

3.2 Zigzag-Aware Sparsity 

To analyze the structural evolution of representa-tions across layers, we model the hidden states of an LVLM as a sequence of point clouds. Given an input token sequence, the hidden representation at layer Lℓ is denoted as HLℓ ∈ RN ×d, where N

is the number of tokens and d is the hidden di-mension. Each token embedding is treated as a point in a d-dimensional space, allowing the layer representation to be interpreted geometrically. To characterize representation evolution across layers, we construct a k-nearest neighbor graph over the point cloud HLℓ , where k is a fixed hyperparameter shared across layers. Based on this graph, we build a simplicial complex KLℓ via clique expansion, where a simplex is included if all its vertices are pairwise connected. To explic-itly link adjacent layers, we introduce intersec-tion layers that capture simplices shared between consecutive complexes. For each adjacent pair 

Lℓ and Lℓ+1 , we define an intersection complex 

KLℓ,L ℓ+1 = KLℓ ∩ K Lℓ+1 , consisting of simplices persisting across both layers. As illustrated by the lightgray component in Figure 2, a quadrangle formed by points x3, x 4, x N −1, x N −2 in Layer 1 reduces to the edge xN −1, x N −2 in the intersection layer, which then merges with another component in Layer 2. 4.1.2 Baseline Methods 

To evaluate the proposed topology-aware pruning framework, we compare it with representative prun-ing methods commonly adopted in LVLMs, cover-ing both similarity-based layer pruning and signal-driven paradigms. Specifically, LLM-Pruner (Ma et al., 2023) and LLM-Streamline (Chen et al., 2024c) are included as similarity-based approaches originally developed for unimodal LLMs and ex-tended to multimodal settings in our experiments. 4.2 Main Results and Analysis 4.2.1 Comparisons on LLaVA-NeXT Model 

Table 1 reports LLaVA-NeXT performance at a 50% sparsity ratio. Our method achieves the high-est overall relative score of 91.6%, surpassing the strongest baseline, TAMP, by 0.7 percentage points. It ranks first on five of seven benchmarks, includ-ing MME Cognition, MME Perception, MMMU, MathVista, and MMBench, while remaining com-petitive on ChartQA and AI2D. Figure 3 further shows that our proposed framework consistently outperforms all baselines across different sparsity ratios, particularly beyond 60% sparsity. In con-trast, methods such as Wanda, OWL and ECoFLaP exhibit more pronounced performance degradation. This advantage stems from topology-aware prun-ing, which identifies and preserves layers that play critical roles in maintaining topological continu-ity across depth, thereby mitigating performance degradation under high sparsity. 

4.2.2 Comparisons on VideoLLaMA2 Model 

Table 2 reports pruning results on VideoLLaMA2 at a 60% sparsity ratio. Our topology-aware ap-proach achieves a relative performance score of 96.7%, outperforming strong baselines such as TAMP, OWL, and SparseGPT. This gain arises from preserving layers that are topologically crit-ical for representation transitions across process-ing stages, which supports stable integration of visual and linguistic information and is often dis-rupted by conventional pruning methods (Frantar and Alistarh, 2023; Sun et al., 2023; Sung et al., 2023). Figure 3 further shows that our method maintains a clear performance advantage as spar-sity increases, with substantially slower degrada-tion compared to competing approaches. This ro-bustness reflects topology-aware pruning that selec-tively retains transition-critical layers rather than relying on static or local criteria, enabling reliable cross-modal interactions under high sparsity (Ye et al., 2023, 2024). Additional experiments on LLaVA-OneVision (Li et al., 2024b), which sup-ports interleaved image and video inputs, are re-ported in Appendix C. 

4.2.3 Comparisons of Inference Speed 

In this section, we provide wall-clock latency, peak VRAM usage, and search-stage efficiency com-parisons, including preprocessing-based baselines such as LaCo (Yang et al., 2024). All results are measured on 8× NVIDIA A100 (80GB) GPUs us-ing LLaVA-NeXT (8B) at 50% sparsity with 512 calibration samples. As shown in Table 3, our method completes the search phase in 5.7 minutes with a single calibration pass, which is substantially faster than reconstruction- or calibration-heavy ap-proaches ( e.g., SparseGPT, Wanda, and LaCo-style preprocessing) that require multiple forward passes and layer-wise reconstruction. Zigzag filtration 

is performed offline and introduces no inference-time overhead. From a complexity perspective, our method only incurs sparse k-nearest neighbor graph construction and 0-1 dimensional zigzag per-sistent homology per layer, resulting in complexity that scales linearly with the number of layers (see Appendix A). It avoids Hessian estimation, back-propagation, and iterative reconstruction, which ex-plains the reduced search-stage runtime. At deploy-ment time, 50% sparsity reduces peak VRAM by 43% and lowers inference latency from 105.4 ms to 60.3 ms, yielding a 1.75 × wall-clock speedup.

4.3.1 Effectiveness of Key Components 

We conduct an ablation study to assess the contri-bution of individual components in our proposed layer pruning framework, with results summarized in Table 4. The unpruned models serve as perfor-mance upper bounds. The baseline adopts uniform pruning, representing a purely local and static selec-tion strategy, which incurs substantial performance degradation and retains only 86.0% and 91.0% of the original performance on LLaVA-NeXT and VideoLLaMA2, respectively, highlighting the lim-itations of disregarding inter-layer structural im-portance. Introducing Topology Scoring incorpo-rates a global yet static view by evaluating layer redundancy through simplicial complexes , improv-ing performance to 87.5% on LLaVA-NeXT and indicating that topological structures provide more informative guidance than isolated pairwise sim-ilarities. Furthermore, Zigzag Tracking captures the dynamic evolution of persistent homological features within layer sequences. Combining these components, the full system achieves global and dynamic topology awareness, attaining superior relative performance of 91.6% on LLaVA-NeXT and 96.7% on VideoLLaMA2. Regarding efficiency, the full system introduces a marginal latency overhead of 2.1 ms due to topo-logical computations, which is outweighed by the gains in model fidelity for lightweight LVLMs.

Limitations 

While the proposed topology-aware pruning frame-work achieves consistent improvements in model efficiency and inference latency, the current study does not explicitly consider hardware-aware co-design for energy efficiency. In practical deploy-ment scenarios, the actual energy savings of pruned LVLMs are strongly influenced by hardware char-acteristics such as memory access patterns, parallel execution efficiency, and device-specific schedul-ing strategies, which are not directly optimized in our framework. As a result, the reduction in model depth and parameter count may not always trans-late proportionally into lower energy consumption across diverse hardware platforms. Incorporating topology-aware pruning into a hardware-conscious optimization pipeline that jointly accounts for ar-chitectural structure and system-level constraints remains an important direction for future work.

A Complexity Analysis 

In this section, we analyze the computational com-plexity of the proposed layer pruning framework. For each layer, we construct a k-nearest neighbor graph over the token-level point cloud HLℓ with N

points, containing O(N k ) edges, where k ≪ N .Based on this graph, 0- and 1-dimensional sim-plicial complexes are built via clique expansion, avoiding high-dimensional simplices. For adja-cent layers, the intersection complex KLℓ,L ℓ+1 re-tains only shared simplices, typically smaller than the individual layer complexes. The overall per-layer computational cost is dominated by k-nearest neighbor construction and union-find operations for connected components and low-order cycles, resulting in a complexity of O(N k ), which remains tractable for large-scale LVLMs. 

B Details of Experimental Setups 

B.1 Calibration Data 

Consistent with common practices in post-training pruning studies (Sun et al., 2023; Frantar and Alis-tarh, 2023; Sung et al., 2023; Zheng et al., 2026b, 2025b; Zhang et al., 2026c,a; Zheng et al., 2026a; Zhang et al., 2024), we construct a lightweight cal-ibration set by randomly sampling 128 instances from the training corpus associated with each tar-get model. Specifically, ShareGPT4V (Chen et al., 2024a) is adopted for calibrating LLaVA-NeXT. For VideoLLaMA2, we select MUSIC-QA (Li et al., 2022) as the calibration dataset, as it naturally incorporates both visual and auditory information. In the case of LLaVA-OneVision (Li et al., 2024b), NLVR2 (Suhr et al., 2019) is employed, since it represents the largest subset of the model’s original training data. 

B.2 Evaluation Protocol 

All quantitative evaluations are conducted using the LMMs-Eval benchmark suite (Zhang et al., 2025b) together with the official evaluation pipelines re-leased by the corresponding model authors, en-suring fair and reproducible comparisons. For LLaVA-NeXT and LLaVA-OneVision, we strictly adhere to the prompt formats provided in the offi-cial LMMs-Eval repository. For VideoLLaMA2, we re-implement its architecture within the LMMs-Eval framework and evaluate it on both audio-centric and video-centric benchmarks.

This synergy allows the proposed layer pruning framework to identify a common redundancy space that transcends individ-ual modalities. By dynamically tracking the persis-tent homology of cross-modal representations, the proposed approach ensures that the pruned archi-tecture preserves the essential pathways required for complex multimodal integration, as evidenced by the superior retention of reasoning capabilities compared to uni-modal alternatives. 

F Robustness Validation 

To verify the stability of various pruning frame-works against fluctuations in calibration data, we perform an extensive sensitivity analysis on LLaVA-NeXT at a 50% sparsity level. This eval-uation involves executing three independent trials with distinct, randomly sampled calibration sets to determine the mean performance and associated standard deviation, supplementing the results ini-tially presented in Table 1. As detailed in Table 9, our proposed method-ology consistently leads all baseline competitors, including the highly competitive SparseGPT, by securing the superior average relative performance across the majority of evaluation benchmarks. Be-yond absolute accuracy, Table 10 highlights that our approach yields the lowest variance in most scenarios, indicating a remarkable level of stability. It is particularly noteworthy that the lower bound of our model’s performance exceeds the upper bound of SparseGPT’s results. This observation estab-lishes a statistically significant performance gap, confirming that our advantages are inherent to the algorithm rather than being artifacts of favorable calibration sampling. The minimal standard devia-tions further suggest that our topological pruning criterion is significantly less susceptible to data-induced noise than alternative strategies. Collec-tively, these empirical observations provide com-pelling evidence for the robustness and reliability of our framework in diverse deployment settings.
