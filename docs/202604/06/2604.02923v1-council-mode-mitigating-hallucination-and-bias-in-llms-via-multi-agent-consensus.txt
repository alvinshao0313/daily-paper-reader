ABSTRACT 

Large Language Models (LLMs), particularly those employing Mixture-of-Experts (MoE) archi-tectures, have achieved remarkable capabilities across diverse natural language processing tasks. However, these models frequently suffer from hallucinations—generating plausible but factually incorrect content—and exhibit systematic biases that are amplified by uneven expert activation during inference. In this paper, we propose the Council Mode , a novel multi-agent consensus framework that addresses these limitations by dispatching queries to multiple heterogeneous frontier LLMs in parallel and synthesizing their outputs through a dedicated consensus model. The Council pipeline operates in three phases: (1) an intelligent triage classifier that routes queries based on complexity, (2) parallel expert generation across architecturally diverse models, and (3) a structured consensus synthesis that explicitly identifies agreement, disagreement, and unique findings before producing the final response. We implement and evaluate this architecture within an open-source AI workspace. Our comprehensive evaluation across multiple benchmarks demonstrates that the Council Mode achieves a 35.9% relative reduction in hallucination rates on the HaluEval benchmark and a 7.8-point improvement on TruthfulQA compared to the best-performing individual model, while maintaining significantly lower bias variance across domains. We provide the mathematical formulation of the consensus mechanism, detail the system architecture, and present extensive empirical results with ablation studies.

1 Introduction 

The rapid advancement of Large Language Models (LLMs) has fundamentally transformed the landscape of natural language processing and artificial intelligence [Vaswani et al., 2017]. Models such as GPT-5.4, Claude Opus 4.6, and Gemini 3.1 Pro have demonstrated unprecedented capabilities in reasoning, generation, and comprehension tasks. To scale model capacity without proportionally increasing computational costs during inference, the Mixture-of-Experts (MoE) architecture has emerged as a dominant paradigm [Shazeer et al., 2017, Fedus et al., 2022]. By activating only a subset of expert modules for each input token, MoE models achieve remarkable efficiency while maintaining or exceeding the performance of dense counterparts. Despite these advances, MoE-based LLMs are particularly susceptible to two critical failure modes: hallucination 

and systematic bias . Hallucination refers to the generation of text that is fluent and plausible but factually incorrect or unsupported by the input context [Ji et al., 2023]. In MoE architectures, the sparse routing mechanism can exacerbate

First, the gating network may consistently route certain types of queries to experts that have internalized incorrect correlations during training, a phenomenon known as expert collapse [Zoph et al., 2022]. Second, the limited activation of experts means that the model’s response to a given query is determined by only a fraction of its total parametric knowledge, increasing the likelihood of epistemic gaps. Third, the routing decisions themselves can introduce systematic biases, as the gating function may develop preferences that correlate with surface-level features rather than semantic content [Riquelme et al., 2021]. Traditional approaches to mitigating hallucinations include Retrieval-Augmented Generation (RAG), which grounds model outputs in external knowledge sources, and Reinforcement Learning from Human Feedback (RLHF), which aligns model behavior with human preferences. While effective to varying degrees, these methods fundamentally operate within the confines of a single model’s parametric memory and reasoning capabilities. Recent research has demonstrated that multi-agent debate and consensus mechanisms can significantly enhance reasoning quality and factual accuracy [Du et al., 2023, Liang et al., 2023, Chen et al., 2023]. The key insight is that different models, trained on different data with different architectures, develop complementary knowledge representations and reasoning strategies. When these models are queried independently and their outputs are systematically compared, idiosyncratic errors tend to be identified and corrected through cross-verification. In this paper, we introduce the Council Mode , a structured multi-agent consensus framework designed to mitigate hallucinations and biases in LLM outputs. Rather than relying on a single model—whether dense or MoE-based—the Council Mode queries N architecturally diverse frontier models in parallel and employs a dedicated synthesis model to aggregate their responses through a formalized consensus protocol. The synthesis process explicitly categorizes the combined knowledge into four structured sections: consensus points, disagreements, unique findings, and a comprehensive analysis. This structured approach ensures that the final output is not merely a majority vote but a nuanced integration that preserves valuable minority insights while filtering out individual model hallucinations. Our implementation is integrated into an open-source AI workspace [Wu, 2026] that serves as the experimental platform for this study. Through extensive experimentation, we evaluate the Council Mode against individual frontier models on established hallucination and reasoning benchmarks. Our main contributions are as follows: 1. We propose the Council Mode, a three-phase multi-agent consensus framework (Triage → Parallel Expert Generation 

→ Consensus Synthesis) that systematically reduces hallucination and bias in LLM outputs. 2. We formalize the mathematical framework for the consensus synthesis mechanism, including the triage function, parallel dispatch protocol, and structured aggregation. 3. We provide a complete open-source implementation and comprehensive empirical evaluation demonstrating signifi-cant improvements over single-model baselines. 4. We conduct ablation studies analyzing the contribution of each pipeline component and the effect of expert diversity on consensus quality. 

2 Related Work 

2.1 Hallucination in Large Language Models 

Hallucination in LLMs has been extensively studied across multiple dimensions. Ji et al. [2023] provide a comprehensive taxonomy distinguishing between intrinsic hallucination (output contradicting the source) and extrinsic hallucination 

(output that cannot be verified from the source). The causes of hallucination are multifaceted, including training data noise, exposure bias during autoregressive generation, and the fundamental limitation of compressing world knowledge into finite parameters [Zhang et al., 2023]. In the context of MoE models, hallucination risks are amplified by the sparse activation pattern. Zoph et al. [2022] demonstrated that MoE models can develop routing biases where certain experts become over-specialized, leading to knowledge gaps when queries fall outside their narrow expertise. Furthermore, the inconsistency between expert routing during training and inference—particularly under policy updates in reinforcement learning—can induce abrupt shifts in the active parameter subspace, destabilizing output quality [DeepSeek-AI, 2025]. 

2.2 Multi-Agent Debate and Consensus 

The use of multiple LLM agents to improve output quality has gained significant traction. Du et al. [2023] introduced multi-agent debate, where multiple instances of the same model argue and refine their answers through multiple 2COUNCIL MODE : M ITIGATING HALLUCINATION AND BIAS VIA MULTI -A GENT CONSENSUS TECHNICAL REPORT 

rounds. Liang et al. [2023] extended this concept with divergent thinking mechanisms. Chen et al. [2023] formalized multi-agent consensus seeking via LLMs, drawing parallels to classical consensus protocols in distributed systems. More recently, Xu et al. [2025] demonstrated that multi-agent architectures can transform hallucination-prone outputs into consensus-based reliable results in software testing. Our Council Mode differs from prior work in several key aspects: (1) we use heterogeneous models from different providers rather than multiple instances of the same model, maximizing cognitive diversity; (2) we employ a structured synthesis protocol that explicitly categorizes consensus, disagreement, and unique findings rather than simple majority voting; and (3) we integrate an intelligent triage mechanism to optimize computational resources by bypassing the full council for trivial queries. 

2.3 Ensemble and Voting Methods 

Classical ensemble methods in machine learning aggregate predictions from multiple models to reduce variance and improve robustness [Dietterich, 2000]. In the LLM context, Wang et al. [2022] proposed self-consistency, which samples multiple reasoning paths from a single model and selects the most consistent answer. Nair-Kanneganti et al. [2025] extended this to voting ensembles across different LLMs. Our approach goes beyond simple voting by employing a synthesis model that performs deep semantic analysis of expert responses, preserving the reasoning chains and evidence that support each claim.

4 Implementation 

The Council Mode is implemented within an open-source AI workspace designed for cloud-native deployment on serverless infrastructure. The implementation is built with Next.js 16 (App Router), React 19, and communicates with the client via a custom Server-Sent Events (SSE) protocol that streams the real-time reasoning states of all parallel experts.

6 Discussion 

6.1 Why Heterogeneous Experts Outperform Homogeneous Ensembles 

The ablation study reveals that the same-model ensemble ( 3× GPT-5.4) achieves an 18.3% relative reduction in hallucination compared to the single GPT-5.4 model, while the heterogeneous Council achieves 35.9%. This disparity arises because models from the same provider share training data, architectural biases, and failure modes. When GPT-5.4 hallucinates on a claim, another instance of GPT-5.4 is likely to produce the same hallucination. In contrast, Claude Opus 4.6 and Gemini 3.1 Pro, trained on different corpora with different architectures, are more likely to provide correct information or at least different errors, enabling the synthesis model to identify and correct the hallucination. 

6.2 The Role of Structured Synthesis 

Simple majority voting treats all claims as binary (correct/incorrect) and discards valuable reasoning chains. The structured synthesis approach preserves the full context of each expert’s response, enabling the synthesis model to: (1) identify why experts disagree, not just that they disagree; (2) assess the strength of evidence supporting each claim; and (3) preserve unique insights that might be lost in a voting scheme. This is particularly important for complex, multi-faceted questions where the “correct” answer is nuanced. 

6.3 Limitations 

The Council Mode has several limitations. First, the increased latency (approximately 2–3 × compared to individual models) may be unacceptable for latency-sensitive applications. Second, the approach relies on the availability and cost of multiple frontier model APIs, which may not be feasible for all deployments. Third, while the consensus mechanism reduces hallucination, it cannot eliminate it entirely—if all experts share the same misconception, the Council will reproduce it. 7 Conclusion 

In this paper, we presented the Council Mode, a multi-agent consensus architecture designed to mitigate hallucinations and biases inherent in traditional LLMs and MoE models. By leveraging the cognitive diversity of multiple heteroge-neous frontier models and employing a structured synthesis pipeline that explicitly identifies consensus, disagreement, and unique findings, the Council Mode delivers highly reliable and factually grounded responses. Our comprehensive evaluation demonstrates significant improvements across multiple benchmarks: a 35.9% relative reduction in halluci-nation rates on HaluEval, a 7.8-point improvement on TruthfulQA, and an 85–89% reduction in bias variance across domains. The open-source implementation provides a robust foundation for future research into multi-model orchestration. We believe that the Council paradigm represents a promising direction for building trustworthy AI systems, complementing rather than replacing ongoing efforts in model-level alignment and safety.

A Synthesis Prompt Template 

The synthesis model receives a system prompt containing 16 mandatory rules. The key rules include: 1. The output must contain exactly four sections: Model Consensus, Model Disagreement, Unique Findings, and Comprehensive Analysis. 2. Each consensus point must be supported by evidence from all three experts. 3. Disagreements must include the reasoning behind each expert’s position. 4. The Comprehensive Analysis must directly answer the user’s question, integrating all evidence. 5. Citations from expert responses must be preserved and attributed. 6. The synthesis must not introduce new claims not present in any expert response. 7. When experts unanimously agree, the synthesis should present the claim with high confidence. 8. When experts disagree, the synthesis should present all perspectives and indicate the weight of evidence.
