## ABSTRACT 

We present FormalProofBench , a private 1 benchmark designed to evaluate whether AI models can produce formally verified mathematical proofs at the grad-uate level. Each task pairs a natural-language problem with a Lean 4 formal state-ment, and a model must output a Lean proof accepted by the Lean 4 checker. For-malProofBench targets advanced undergraduate and graduate mathematics, with problems drawn from qualifying exams and standard textbooks across topics in-cluding analysis, algebra, probability, and logic. We evaluate a range of fron-tier models with an agentic harness, and find that the best-performing foundation model achieves 33.5% accuracy, with performance dropping rapidly after that. In addition to the accuracy numbers, we also provide empirical analysis of tool-use, failure modes, cost and latency, thereby providing a thorough evaluation of the formal-theorem proving abilities of frontier models. 

## 1 INTRODUCTION 

Large language models have made striking progress on mathematical reasoning. Recent systems achieve high scores on competition-style benchmarks such as MATH (Hendrycks et al., 2021), GSM8K (Cobbe et al., 2021), the International Mathematical Olympiad, and more challenging math benchmarks like FrontierMath (Besiroglu et al., 2024), driven by advances in pre-training on math-ematical corpora, post-training with reinforcement learning from human and automated feedback, and inference-time reasoning. However, natural-language solutions can hide subtle logical errors that are difficult to audit by both human and LLM-based graders. A model may produce a plausible-sounding argument that omits a critical case or invokes a non-existent lemma, errors that even expert reviewers can overlook. This gap between apparent and verified mathematical competence motivates the use of formal verification as an evaluation methodology: a proof either type-checks or it does not. Independently, the mathematics community has increasingly adopted interactive theorem provers. Lean 4 (de Moura et al., 2021) and its mathematical library Mathlib (mathlib Community, 2020) have experienced rapid growth, and formalization efforts such as the Liquid Tensor Experi-ment (Commelin & Topaz, 2023) and the ongoing formalization of Fermat’s Last Theorem (Buzzard & Taylor, 2025) have demonstrated that modern proof assistants can scale to deep, research-level mathematics. Concurrently, several recent works have explored the intersection of AI and formal mathematics, including Google DeepMind’s AlphaProof (Hubert et al., 2025) and the Aristotle sys-tem for auto-formalization (Achim et al., 2025). These developments underscore both the oppor-tunity and the need for rigorous benchmarks that measure whether language models can produce machine-checkable proofs for non-trivial mathematics.

We present FormalProofBench , a private benchmark designed to evaluate formal theorem-proving capability at the advanced undergraduate and graduate level. Problems are drawn from qualifying exams and standard text-books spanning measure theory, real and functional analysis, algebra and commutative algebra, al-gebraic geometry, number theory, probability and stochastic analysis, and logic. ## 2 RELATED WORK 

Informal mathematics benchmarking Natural language mathematics (which we refer to as “in-formal”) have been widely used in evaluating and guiding LLM training. Two widely adopted benchmarks are MATH (Hendrycks et al., 2021), which consists of 12,500 competition-mathematics problems, and AIME, a popular benchmark measuring how well models do on American Invitational Mathematics Competition problems. More recently, benchmarks such as IMProofBench and Fron-tierMath have begun evaluating models on harder, research-level tasks. 

Formal mathematics benchmarks Several benchmarks evaluate neural theorem provers at dif-ferent difficulty levels. MiniF2F (Zheng et al., 2022) is a cross-system benchmark containing olympiad-level problems formalized in multiple proof assistants. PutnamBench (Tsoukalas et al., 2024) extends this to collegiate competition mathematics, providing Putnam problems in Lean, Coq, and Isabelle. ProofNet (Azerbayev et al., 2023) targets undergraduate-level theorem proving and autoformalization in Lean 3. FormalMATH (Yu et al., 2025) offers a larger-scale benchmark that also addresses undergraduate mathematics. Domain-specific benchmarks address particular mathematical subfields. IneqComp (Zhao et al., 2025) tests compositional reasoning with mathematical inequalities. CombiBench (Liu et al., 2025) focuses on combinatorial mathematics with 100 competition problems formalized in Lean 4. MATP-Bench (He et al., 2025) evaluates the capability of models in multi-modal reasoning in a formal setting. 

Formal theorem proving On the methods side, autoformalization with LLMs (Wu et al., 2022) demonstrated that large language models can translate natural-language mathematics into formal specifications. GoedelProver (Lin et al., 2025) builds on this by training iteratively on autoformal-ized proofs, achieving strong results on MiniF2F and PutnamBench. LeanDojo v2 (Hsiang et al., 2025) provides an end-to-end framework for AI-assisted theorem proving in Lean 4, unifying repos-itory extraction, LLM fine-tuning, and proof search. Lean Workbook (Ying et al., 2025) provides approximately 57,000 formal-informal problem pairs, including IMO questions, generated via an iterative translation pipeline. SorryDB (Letson et al., 2026) indexes incomplete proofs (“sorries”) from public Lean repositories, serving as a live benchmark based on publicly available unresolved sorries. FormalProofBench aims to evaluate models’ formal theorem-proving abilities on tasks that are at the advanced undergraduate or graduate level, as opposed to high-school problems. In addition, we evaluate models with an agentic scaffold, instead of evaluating their ability to generate the proof in one single forward pass. 

## 3 BENCHMARK OVERVIEW 

Each task in FormalProofBench consists of (i) a mathematical problem in natural-language, and (ii) the corresponding formal statement in Lean 4. Given both, a model must construct a Lean proof accepted by the proof checker. 3.1 DATA CURATION 

Problems were selected and formalized by a total of six formalizers who were PhD students or ex-perts experienced in writing Lean 4 code. All of them had significant prior experience writing Lean, either via merged contributions to Mathlib, or prior large-scale formalization effort that they con-tributed to, and had varied mathematical interests, enabling us to formalize problems from different fields. Our problem-collection process used the following criteria: • Problems that required knowledge beyond olympiad-style / high-school style questions. • Problems that could be formalized using current Mathlib, and where the proof could also be written using current Mathlib. • Problems that would challenge a reasonably capable model as of late 2025, while still being relevant to mathematical work in that domain. 3Published as a conference paper at ICLR 2026 • A diversity of mathematical domains. For examples of a task see Listing 1; full model responses for this problem and others are given in Appendix A.1. 

Problem sources and domains The problems are drawn from advanced undergraduate and gradu-ate sources, including qualifying exams and standard textbooks. They span a broad range of modern mathematics, including measure theory, real and functional analysis, algebra and commutative alge-bra, algebraic geometry, number theory, probability and stochastic analysis, and logic/model theory. Data quality and misformalization checks While Lean 4 can act as a checker of whether a proof indeed proves a statement, a common problem in formal math benchmarks is misformalization of the problem statement , due to misaligned definitions, or incorrect translation to Lean 4. In other words, it is not guaranteed whether the formal statement faithfully expresses the intent of the infor-mal statement. In addition, it is well-known that formal benchmarks often have issues, including misformalized problems and omitted side conditions (Ammanamanchi & Bhat, 2025). Anticipating such issues, our data collection process included a secondary review phase of human review and revisions to problem statements. The secondary review phase was performed using standard code review processes on GitHub, and involved a review of the informal mathematics statement, natural language proof, and the formal statement by one or more people other than the original contributor of the problem. 

Necessity of a search tool Some problems in the benchmark have solutions that depend on a theorem that was recently added to mathlib . Although models could in principle reprove any needed lemmas, we include these problems to test a crucial capability for real-world formalization: reliably looking up relevant theorems. 3.2 EVALUATION SETTINGS 

A common problem with evaluations is that different models have different settings, such as rea-soning effort, temperature, top_p , inference provider, etc. We standardize our evaluation settings by using the default sampling settings from each model provider, and by using the native model provider rather than a third-party inference provider.

The average cost per test ranges from around 10 cents to more than a dollar. ## 6 CONCLUSION AND FUTURE WORK 

We present FormalProofBench, a benchmark evaluating whether AI models can produce formally verified mathematical proofs at the graduate level. Our evaluation of frontier models reveals that the best-performing system, Claude Opus 4.5 (Thinking), achieves 33.5% accuracy, with performance dropping rapidly thereafter. Through detailed analysis of tool use patterns, we find that frequent iter-ation via code execution correlates strongly with success, while excessive search without execution often indicates a failing trajectory. 

A benchmark at the intersection of multiple desiderata. FormalProofBench occupies a unique position in the landscape of AI evaluation. Recent benchmarks such as FrontierMath prioritize including challenging problems of real world significance. FormalProofBench does this but also offers several additional advantages that are difficult to achieve simultaneously: (i) deterministic evaluation via the Lean 4 kernel eliminates the need for model-graded or human-graded correctness checks, (ii) contamination resistance, as the specific Lean formalizations we created are unlikely to appear in training data, (iii) practical evaluation costs, with per-problem evaluation completing in minutes to hours rather than days, and (iv) solvability, since we chose problems that can be correctly stated/solved in Lean using current Mathlib. This combination makes FormalProofBench particularly valuable for tracking progress as models continue to improve. 8Published as a conference paper at ICLR 2026 

Implications for mathematical practice. The gap between informal and formal mathematical competence reveals that formalization remains a significant bottleneck. However, the rapid pace of improvement across recent model releases suggests we may be approaching a threshold where AI systems become practical tools for working mathematicians. Success at formal verification could en-able rapid exploration of proof strategies with immediate correctness feedback, democratize access to formal methods beyond the small community of proof assistant experts, and accelerate the for-malization of research-level mathematics. As performance on benchmarks like FormalProofBench continues to improve, formal verification may transition from a specialized technique to a standard component of the mathematical research toolkit. 

Future directions. Several extensions would deepen our understanding of model capabilities: 1. Exploring alternative tool configurations: The lean_loogle tool was significantly less useful to models than the lean_run_code tool, and we observed that there were a non-trivial number of trajectories where models unsuccessfully over-used the Loogle tool. Alternative configurations including semantic search, the ability to grep local codebases, and tactic-level tools may reveal whether the current search interface is the bottleneck or whether deeper reasoning limitations persist. 2. Ablating the informal problem statement: We currently provide both the natural lan-guage statement and formal statement to models. Analyzing how performance changes with and without informal proof access would clarify whether models struggle primarily with proof generation or with translating mathematical ideas into Lean syntax. 3. Broader diversity and coverage: Our benchmark consists of 200 problems. Expanding coverage to additional mathematical domains and increasing problem count would improve statistical power and enable domain-specific analysis. 4. Evaluating research-level formal theorem proving: While FormalProofBench considers advanced undergraduate and graduate level mathematics, we were constrained by the cur-rent contents of Mathlib in what we could plausibly formalize. As more mathematics becomes formalized and available in Mathlib , future iterations of this benchmark could target research-level theorems, providing a pathway to evaluate AI systems on problems at the frontier of mathematical knowledge.
