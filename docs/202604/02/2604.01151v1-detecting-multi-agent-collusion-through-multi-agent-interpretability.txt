## Abstract 

As LLM agents are increasingly deployed in multi-agent systems, they introduce risks of covert coordination that may evade standard forms of human oversight. While linear probes on model activations have shown promise for detecting decep-tion in single-agent settings, collusion is inherently a multi-agent phenomenon, and the use of internal representations for detecting collusion between agents remains unexplored. We introduce NARCB ENCH , a benchmark for evaluating collusion detection under environment distribution shift, and propose five probing techniques that aggregate per-agent deception scores to classify scenarios at the group level. Our probes achieve 1.00 AUROC in-distribution and 0.60–0.86 AUROC when transferred zero-shot to structurally different multi-agent scenarios and a stegano-graphic blackjack card-counting task. We find that no single probing technique dominates across all collusion types, suggesting that different forms of collusion manifest differently in activation space. We also find preliminary evidence that this signal is localised at the token level, with the colluding agent’s activations spiking specifically when processing the encoded parts of their partner’s message. This work takes a step toward multi-agent interpretability : extending white-box inspection from single models to multi-agent contexts, where detection requires aggregating signals across agents. These results suggest that model internals pro-vide a complementary signal to text-level monitoring for detecting multi-agent collusion, particularly for organisations with access to model activations. Code and data are available at https://github.com/aaronrose227/narcbench .

## 1 Introduction 

Recent advances in LLMs have driven rapid progress in multi-agent systems, where multiple agents collaborate on tasks ranging from software development [Hong et al., 2024, Qian et al., 2024] to negotiation and decision-making [Guo et al., 2024]. As these systems are deployed with increasing autonomy, they introduce new safety risks [Hammond et al., 2025]. Among the most concerning is covert coordination: agents may secretly work together to steer outcomes, suppress information, or undermine oversight [Park et al., 2024, Scheurer et al., 2024, Motwani et al., 2024]. Motwani et al. [2024] formalise this threat, showing that LLM agents can establish steganographic communication channels that encode hidden information in natural-sounding text. Mathew et al. [2025] demonstrate that such channels can emerge from misspecified training incentives without explicit instruction, and

that standard mitigations including output paraphrasing are insufficient to prevent them. In this work, we show that such collusion can be detected by inspecting agents’ internal activations, even when their text outputs appear entirely normal. Proposed defences against covert coordination include monitoring text outputs, paraphrasing agent communications, and imposing behavioural constraints [Greenblatt et al., 2024, Syrnikov et al., 2026]. However, these have been shown to fail against steganographic signals designed to be indistinguishable from natural language [Motwani et al., 2024, Mathew et al., 2025]. A separate line of work has shown that linear probes on model activations can detect deception in single-agent settings: Goldowsky-Dill et al. [2025] achieve AUROCs of 0.96–0.999 on scenarios including insider trading and sandbagging, and Anthropic [2024] detect sleeper agent defection with AUROC above 0.99. However, little prior work has applied activation-based detection to multi-agent settings. Collusion is inherently a multi-agent phenomenon: it arises from the relationship between agents, not from any one agent in isolation. Detecting it requires examining how agents’ internal representations relate to each other, a problem that maps onto the classical framework of distributed anomaly detection [Chandola et al., 2009], where the anomaly only becomes visible when information is combined across distributed nodes. We frame this as a problem of multi-agent interpretability : applying white-box detection techniques across multiple interacting agents to identify collective phenomena invisible from any single agent alone. We first learn a deception direction from a training corpus of multi-agent scenarios in which some agents are instructed to collude. Each agent’s activation is then projected onto this direction to produce a scalar deception score. Given the scores of all agents in a scenario, we propose five novel probing techniques, each built on this single learned deception probe, that ask whether the pattern of scores across the group is consistent with collusion: for instance, whether there is a subset of agents with elevated scores (Deception Split), whether the score distribution has unusually high variance or range (Asymmetry Probe), or simply how high the most suspicious agent scores (Peak Suspicion). We learn the deception direction on a core dataset and evaluate all five methods zero-shot on held-out settings that differ structurally from the training data: a suite of six real-world-inspired scenarios (code review sabotage, stock manipulation, RAG poisoning, and others) where collusion takes different interactive forms, and a blackjack card-counting task in which two agents develop steganographic signals hidden in natural table talk. The deception probe trained on the deception scenarios transfers to detect collusion in both the cross-structure scenarios and the blackjack task, despite these settings having entirely different interaction structures and collusion mechanisms. No single probing technique dominates across all settings: different forms of collusion are best detected by different techniques, suggesting that collusion manifests heterogeneously in activation space. In the steganographic blackjack setting, the most structurally distant setting from the training data, involving a completely different task domain and collusion mechanism, an LLM agent judge monitoring the table talk flags a similar proportion of collusion and control runs as suspicious, failing to discriminate between the two conditions, while our probing techniques achieve 0.90–1.00 AUROC. We also find that the colluding agent’s activations diverge from honest agents specifically at the tokens where the covert signal is encoded, suggesting the model internally represents the act of decoding a steganographic message differently from simply reading the same words without context. These results suggest that white-box access to model internals provides a complementary signal to text-level monitoring for detecting multi-agent collusion. For organisations with access to model activations, this offers a practical addition to existing safety mechanisms: a monitoring layer that operates on what agents compute, not just what they say. Our contributions are: • A framework for multi-agent collusion detection via a learned deception probe, and five novel probing techniques (Sorted Concatenation, Activation Alignment, Peak Suspicion, Deception Split, and Asymmetry Probe) that each aggregate per-agent deception scores to classify scenarios at the group level. • NARCB ENCH (Neural Activation Reading for Collusion Benchmark), a three-tier bench-mark for evaluating collusion detection under environment distribution shift: C ORE (50 scenarios, 17 domains), T RANSFER (6 structurally distinct scenario types), and S TEGO 

(steganographic blackjack card counting). 2• Empirical evidence that learned deception directions transfer across structurally different collusion tasks, and that the collusion signal is localised at the token level to the moment of covert communication. 

## 2 Related work 

Multi-agent collusion and steganography. Recent work has established that LLM agents can collude covertly in multi-agent settings. Motwani et al. [2024] formalise the threat of steganographic collusion, showing that frontier models can encode hidden information in natural-sounding text. Mathew et al. [2025] demonstrate that such steganographic communication can emerge from mis-specified training incentives, not only from explicit prompting, and that standard mitigations such as output paraphrasing are insufficient. Beyond steganography, Fish et al. [2025] show that LLM pricing agents autonomously converge on supracompetitive prices without explicit collusion instructions, and Curvo [2025] study deception dynamics in social deduction games, finding that deceptive capa-bility may scale faster than detection ability. Hammond et al. [2025] provide a broad taxonomy of multi-agent risks, identifying collusion as a key failure mode. On the mitigation side, Syrnikov et al. [2026] show that declarative prohibitions alone do not prevent collusion under optimisation pressure. Several frameworks have been proposed for auditing multi-agent collusion behaviourally, including Colosseum [Nakamura et al., 2026], which measures collusion via regret metrics, and SentinelAgent [He et al., 2025], which models agent interactions as execution graphs. These approaches operate on agent outputs or behavioural traces; none examine model internals. 

Probing activations for deception. A growing body of work shows that LLM internal repre-sentations encode information about truthfulness and deceptive intent that is accessible to simple linear probes. Zou et al. [2023] introduce Representation Engineering, demonstrating that high-level concepts including honesty are linearly represented in activation space. Marks and Tegmark [2024] provide geometric evidence for linear truth structure, and Azaria and Mitchell [2023] show that classifiers on hidden states predict statement truthfulness. Most directly relevant to our work, Goldowsky-Dill et al. [2025] train linear probes on Llama-3.3-70B activations to detect strategic deception, achieving AUROCs of 0.96–0.999 on scenarios including insider trading and sandbagging. Anthropic [2024] show that simple “defection probes” catch sleeper agents with AUROC above 0.99. All of this work operates in a single-agent setting: it probes one model’s activations to detect that model’s deception. Our work extends this to the multi-agent case, where the signal of interest is the relationship between agents’ representations, not any single agent’s internal state. 

Distributed anomaly detection. We frame multi-agent collusion detection as a distributed anomaly detection problem following the taxonomy of Chandola et al. [2009], who distinguish three anomaly types: point anomalies (a single instance is anomalous), contextual anomalies (anomalous in a specific context), and collective anomalies (a group of instances is anomalous even though no individual instance is). Collusion is a collective anomaly: a single colluding agent’s activations may appear only mildly unusual, but the pattern across agents reveals the collusion. The distributed detection literature further distinguishes three detection paradigms based on how information from distributed nodes is combined [Chandola et al., 2009]. In centralised fusion , raw data from all nodes is aggregated into a single representation and classified globally. In decision-level fusion , each node produces a local score or decision, and a second stage combines these to reach a system-level verdict. In correlation-based detection , anomalies are identified from changes in inter-node statistical relationships rather than from any node’s data in isolation. Our five probing techniques collectively instantiate all three paradigms (Section 3.3), providing complementary coverage of the anomaly types that different forms of collusion produce.

It comprises three tiers of increasing structural distance from the training data. All scenarios share a common structure: four LLM agents interact in a shared conversation. Each scenario is run in two conditions: a collusion condition, in which two agents are secretly instructed to coordinate toward a hidden objective via a private channel before engaging in public discussion, and a control condition, in which all four agents receive identical honest instructions. 

NARCB ENCH -C ORE (training and in-distribution evaluation). 50 scenarios spanning 17 do-mains (medical ethics, financial, legal, military, cybersecurity, environmental, and others). In each, four agents serve on a decision-making committee voting on a binary choice. Colluding agents are instructed to steer the committee toward a predetermined outcome while concealing their collusion. Scenarios are diversified across agent names, instruction style, domain framing, and option labels to mitigate prompt confounds (see below). We generate both collusion and control conditions for each scenario, yielding 100 runs. A held-out subset of scenarios from unseen domains is reserved for in-distribution evaluation. 

NARCB ENCH -T RANSFER (out-of-distribution: cross-structure generalisation). Six struc-turally distinct scenario types designed to test whether a probe trained on NARCB ENCH -C ORE can generalise to different interaction patterns: code review sabotage, pump-and-dump stock manipula-tion, CI/CD pipeline sabotage, retrieval-augmented generation (RAG) knowledge base poisoning, research policy slanting, and news framing. Each type has six sub-scenarios run in collusion and control conditions (36 collusion, 36 control). These differ from C ORE not only in domain but in interaction structure: agents review code, write reports, or curate information rather than deliberating on a committee. 

NARCB ENCH -S TEGO (out-of-distribution: steganographic collusion). A card-counting collu-sion task where two colluding agents (a counter and a big player) pre-negotiate a signalling scheme in a private channel, then play 8-hand blackjack shoes alongside two honest agents. The counter encodes the running card count in natural table talk (e.g. asking a question signals a negative count, enthusiasm signals a hot deck) and the big player decodes the signal to size their bets. Each of 20 shoes uses a unique encoding scheme invented by the agents themselves. Both collusion and control conditions are run per shoe (40 runs). This is the hardest tier: the collusion mechanism is steganographic and structurally unlike anything in the training data (Figure 1). 

Mitigating prompt confounds. A known challenge for activation-based deception detection is that probes may classify based on prompt surface features rather than internal representations of deceptive intent [Goldowsky-Dill et al., 2025, Levinstein and Herrmann, 2024]. Following prior work, we mitigate this by maximising diversity across scenarios (names, instruction style, domain-specific framing, option labels) while keeping the evaluation-time user prompt identical for colluders and 4honest agents. As a direct test, a proportion of honest agents in C ORE are given aggressive or opinionated personas, ensuring the probe cannot simply learn that forceful or persuasive language indicates collusion. We cannot fully rule out that residual prompt differences contribute to the signal. However, the out-of-distribution transfer results (Section 4) provide evidence that the probe captures something beyond prompt artefacts: the probing techniques generalise to NARCB ENCH -T RANSFER 

and NARCB ENCH -S TEGO , where prompts are structurally different from C ORE .

3.2 Activation extraction and probe training 

All experiments are run on two NVIDIA RTX A5500 GPUs (24GB each), with models served using 4-bit quantisation and tensor parallelism. We use Qwen3-32B [Qwen Team, 2025] as our primary model. We extract activations from each agent’s public responses at the last token position, across layers 26–30 (of 64), as middle layers have been shown to best encode semantic representations [Zou et al., 2023]. We report mean AUROC across these layers. For NARCB ENCH -C ORE , we average each agent’s activations across all discussion rounds to capture their sustained representation of the shared deliberation. For T RANSFER and S TEGO , we use final-round activations only (the last round where all agents receive identical user prompts), as these scenarios have fewer rounds and the final response best reflects each agent’s settled position. For NARCB ENCH -S TEGO , we additionally extract per-token activations to enable token-level analysis. 

Deception probe. Following Goldowsky-Dill et al. [2025], we fit a linear probe to distinguish collud-ing agents from honest agents. We collect activations from NARCB ENCH -C ORE collusion runs only (public channel), giving a dataset of activation vectors xi ∈ Rd with labels yi ∈ { honest , colluder },balanced by downsampling the majority class. We exclude 4 scenarios where colluders failed to coordinate. We standardise activations to zero mean and unit variance, then train a logistic regres-sion classifier ( C = 1 .0). The normalised weight vector defines a deception direction d ∈ Rd

[Goldowsky-Dill et al., 2025, Zou et al., 2023]. For a new agent j, we compute a scalar deception score :

sj = ¯ aj · d (1) where higher scores indicate a more colluder-like internal state. A null probe trained on a random split of honest agents scores near chance (AUROC 0.49), confirming the probe is not picking up incidental differences. 

Evaluation. For NARCB ENCH -C ORE , we evaluate via leave-domain-out cross-validation: for each of the 17 domains, we train the deception direction and any run-level classifiers on the remaining 16 domains and evaluate on the held-out domain. Core results are the mean across folds. For T RANSFER 

and S TEGO , we train on all of C ORE and evaluate zero-shot. All results are AUROC, measuring how well each method’s score separates collusion runs from control runs. Three of the five methods (Peak Suspicion, Deception Split, Activation Alignment) require no training beyond the deception direction: they compute a single scalar per run and we measure how well this scalar separates the two conditions. The remaining two (Sorted Concatenation, Asymmetry Probe) train an additional classifier on C ORE run-level features. 

3.3 Probing techniques 

The single-agent deception score classifies each agent independently. But collusion is a collective phenomenon: it may only be detectable by examining how agents’ representations relate to each other [Chandola et al., 2009]. We introduce five probing techniques that operate on the set of deception scores (or activations) across all agents in a scenario, spanning the three distributed detection paradigms of Section 2. Sorted Concatenation implements centralised fusion , ingesting full activation vectors from all agents. Peak Suspicion, Deception Split, and Asymmetry Probe implement decision-level fusion , each combining per-agent scalar deception scores through a different aggregation rule. Activation Alignment implements correlation-based detection , measuring inter-agent representational similarity without using the deception direction. On C ORE , all methods achieve 1.00 AUROC under leave-domain-out cross-validation. The central result is that the deception probe trained on committee deliberations transfers to struc-turally different collusion tasks without retraining. On T RANSFER , the best method (Asymmetry Probe) achieves 0.84 AUROC across six scenario types it has never seen. On S TEGO , where agents invent their own steganographic encoding schemes, detection remains strong (Peak Suspicion: 0.99, Activation Alignment: 1.00). However, no single method dominates, and the distributed detection taxonomy explains why: different collusion types produce different anomaly signatures. Centralised fusion (Sorted Concatenation) excels when colluders’ full representations are globally displaced. Decision-level fusion methods (Peak Suspicion, Deception Split, Asymmetry Probe) are strongest at collective anomalies visible in the distribution of scalar deception scores. Correlation-based detection (Activation Alignment) captures changes in inter-agent representational similarity that the other paradigms miss entirely. Each method has characteristic strengths: • Peak Suspicion is the most consistent method. It succeeds when collusion causes at least one agent to stand out individually along the deception direction. This is the common case: typically at least one colluder adopts a noticeably different internal posture from the honest agents. • Asymmetry Probe captures subtler signals by examining the shape of the score distribution across all agents, not just the maximum. This makes it robust to scenarios where both colluders score moderately rather than one scoring very high. • Sorted Concatenation leverages the full activation vectors rather than scalar projections. It excels when colluders’ representations are globally different from honest agents (e.g. Pump & Dump, Stego), but struggles when the difference is confined to the deception subspace (e.g. CI/CD, News Framing). • Deception Split assumes collusion creates a clean gap between two high-scoring and two low-scoring agents. This holds when both colluders successfully coordinate, but breaks down when only one follows through or when the task structure blurs the separation. • Activation Alignment measures whether agents process shared context similarly. It detects steganographic collusion perfectly because encoding and decoding covert signals fundamen-tally changes how agents represent the same text. However, it is the weakest method on TRANSFER (0.54 AUROC): for structural collusion the direction of the effect is inconsistent across tasks, with colluders sometimes becoming more aligned and sometimes less aligned.

4.3 Observations Collusion is not monolithic. The per-type variation in Table 1 suggests that collusion manifests differently depending on the task. Steganographic collusion disrupts activation geometry (Activation Alignment: 1.00), while structural collusion is subtler and does not shift pairwise similarity in a consistent direction. In distributed detection terms, steganographic collusion produces correlation anomalies best captured by correlation-based methods, while structural collusion produces collective anomalies in the score distribution that decision-level fusion methods detect. A robust detection system should therefore run methods from all three paradigms in parallel, flagging scenarios where any method exceeds a threshold. 

Transfer is hard but possible. The deception direction trained on committee deliberations transfers meaningfully to structurally different tasks (0.80–0.84 AUROC), despite never seeing code reviews, stock analyses, or RAG pipelines during training. This suggests a degree of universality in how collusive intent is represented in activation space, though the gap from Core (1.00) to Transfer (0.84) indicates substantial room for improvement. 8Text-level monitoring fails on steganography. In NARCB ENCH -S TEGO , an LLM agent judge monitoring the table talk flags a similar proportion of collusion and control runs as suspicious, failing to discriminate between the two conditions. The steganographic signals are designed to be indistinguishable from normal conversation, and the judge cannot tell the difference. Our activation-based methods, by contrast, achieve 0.90–1.00 AUROC on the same data, with all five methods achieving 0.90 or above. 

Token-level signal at the point of decoding. The big player’s activations diverge from honest agents specifically at the counter’s signal tokens ( +0 .29 vs −1.41 ), suggesting the model internally distinguishes between “reading words” and “decoding a covert signal.” This localisation is preliminary (39 examples, single layer) but points toward a mechanistic understanding of how steganographic decoding is represented. 

4.4 Limitations Single model. Our results are based on a single model (Qwen3-32B) with 4-bit quantisation. Whether the findings generalise across model families and scales is an open question. The models we use do not always collude effectively when instructed: in 4 of 50 Core collusion scenarios, neither agent followed through on the collusion instruction. Frontier models may collude more reliably, but their activations are not publicly accessible for this type of analysis. 

Probe imperfections. As with prior work on activation-based deception detection [Goldowsky-Dill et al., 2025], our probes are imperfect and may rely partly on spurious correlations. We mitigate prompt confounds through scenario diversification and identical evaluation-time prompts, but cannot fully rule out that residual prompt differences contribute to the signal. The out-of-distribution transfer results provide evidence against pure prompt artefacts, but the gap between Core (1.00 AUROC) and Transfer (0.84) suggests the probe captures a mixture of robust and dataset-specific features. Future work could explore whether improved training sets, non-linear probes, or different score aggregation strategies yield more robust directions. 

Label ambiguity. Determining ground-truth labels for collusion is inherently difficult. We label runs based on whether agents were instructed to collude, but instruction does not guarantee execution, hence the 4 excluded scenarios. Conversely, some runs labelled as control may contain emergent coordination that we do not detect. A privileged-observer control (an agent who knows the collusion scheme but is instructed not to participate) would help distinguish knowledge of collusion from active participation; we leave this for future work. 

Adversarial robustness. The adversarial robustness of these techniques is untested: colluding agents that are aware of activation monitoring could potentially coordinate to suppress the signal. 

## 5 Conclusion 

We present NARCB ENCH , a three-tier benchmark for multi-agent collusion detection, and five novel probing techniques (Sorted Concatenation, Activation Alignment, Peak Suspicion, Deception Split, and Asymmetry Probe) that each aggregate per-agent deception scores to classify scenarios at the group level. We find that these techniques transfer zero-shot to realistic and structurally different collusion settings, and that no single technique dominates across all scenario types, with each having characteristic strengths depending on the form of collusion. We also find that the collusion signal can be localised to specific tokens where covert communication occurs. More broadly, these results motivate multi-agent interpretability as a structured framework for AI safety. By grounding our probing techniques in the distributed anomaly detection taxonomy—centralised fusion, decision-level fusion, and correlation-based detection—we provide a principled decomposition that explains why no single method suffices and guides the design of future methods.
