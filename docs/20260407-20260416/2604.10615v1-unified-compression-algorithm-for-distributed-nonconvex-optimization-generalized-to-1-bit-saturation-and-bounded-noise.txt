Abstract 

In this paper, we propose a unified compression algorithm for distributed nonconvex opitmization with both the locally- and globally-bounded communication compressors, including 1-bit compressors, saturating quantizers, and the globally-bounded compressors with both relative and absolute compression errors, as well as additional arbitrary bounded noise. We provide a rigorous convergence analysis in nonconvex settings and establish linear convergence under the Polyak‚ÄìLojasiewicz (P‚ÄìL) condition. Notably, we establish an O(1 /‚àöT ) convergence rate for the locally-bounded class in the distributed nonconvex setting, matching that achieved by the centralized algorithms with 1-bit compressors, where T denotes the total number of iterations. Moreover, one initial uncompressed communication round further yields an order-wise improvement to O(1 /T 2/3). For the P‚ÄìL setting and the globally-bounded class, we recover state-of-the-art convergence rates. 

Key words: Unified compression, distributed nonconvex optimization, 1-bit, saturation, bounded noise

This example illustrates a common challenge in distributed optimization, where communication bandwidth can be severely limited and communication may become a performance bottleneck. To reduce communication overhead, a broad family of communication-efficient methods has been developed, including compression (Rabbat and Nowak, 2005), event-triggered communication (Lemmon, 2010), delay-tolerant designs (Agarwal and Duchi, 2011), and asyn-chronous updates (Nedi¬¥ c, 2011). Among them, com-pression is a widely used approach due to its simplicity and the finite-precision nature of digital hardware. In practice, high-precision communication is costly and perfectly accurate communication is impossible. This motivates the use of a compressor C : Rd ‚Üí Rd that reduces the number of communicated bits by mapping a vector to its compressed representation. Accordingly, one major line of research has focused on developing different forms of compressors. Representative exam-ples studied in distributed convex optimization include quantizers (Yi and Hong, 2014), unbiased compres-sors (Alistarh et al., 2017), and contractive compres-sors (Koloskova et al., 2019), which were later extended to nonconvex settings (Tang et al., 2018). Most existing works consider compressors with a global compression property (hereafter, global compressors), where the compression error ‚à•C (x) ‚àí x‚à• admits a global bound for all x in Rd. In contrast, few works investigate compressors that satisfy only local properties (hereafter, local compressors). A typical example is the 1-bit com-pressor, which transmits only one bit per coordinate and thus conveys extremely limited information. Theoretical analysis for such aggressive compression is usually car-ried out in convex settings and often require relatively strong assumptions to ensure convergence. For exam-ple, Zhang et al. (2023) and Yi et al. (2022) established convergence under strong convexity and the Polyak‚Äì Lojasiewicz (P‚ÄìL) conditions, respectively. Although Yi and Hong (2014) considered convex optimization, it es-tablished convergence without an explicit rate. In non-convex optimization, 1-bit methods are developed under a centralized server paradigm (Zhu et al., 2020; Fan et al., 2022), where the consensus problem does not arise due to a shared initialization and the server-broadcast gradient update. They achieve an O(1 /‚àöT ) convergence rate and rely on relatively strong assumptions such as Lipschitz continuity and homogeneous problem settings, where T

denotes the total number of iterations. Another repre-sentative local compressor is the saturating quantizer. However, many works neglect saturation, effectively ren-dering it a global compressor (Alistarh et al., 2017; Yi et al., 2022). Theoretical guarantees under a given or ar-bitrary saturation level are relatively few and often rely on strong convexity or the P‚ÄìL condition (Magn¬¥ usson et al., 2020; Xu et al., 2024). In the convex setting, Saha et al. (2021) established convergence with proba-bility proportional to the saturation level and a rate of 

O(T ‚àí(1 /2‚àíŒ∏)) for some Œ∏ ‚àà (0 , 1/2). Given that local compressors have been less explored, designing a unified algorithm that handles both local and global compressors is even more challenging. Beyond works on saturating quantizers that also cover the unsat-urated case, to the best of our knowledge, only Yi et al. (2022) provided a unified algorithm applicable to local and global compressors with absolute compression error, albeit under the P‚ÄìL condition. For global compressors, there have been efforts toward unifying compression and algorithmic design. Richt¬¥ arik et al. (2021) proposed an algorithm that works with both unbiased and contrac-tive compressors. Liao et al. (2022) futher considered a more general class of compressors that unifies unbiased, contractive, and certain biased non-contractive compres-sors. More recently, Michelusi et al. (2022); Nassif et al. (2023); Liao et al. (2024) progressively moved toward a unified class of compressors that accommodates both relative and absolute compression errors. As discussed above, theoretical results for local compres-sors remain limited, and a unified treatment of local and global compressors is even less developed. In particu-lar, theoretical guarantees are still lacking for (i) several representative local compressors in nonconvex settings, including 1-bit compressors and saturating quantizers, (ii) a unified treatment of local and global compressors in nonconvex settings, and (iii) a unified algorithm ap-plicable to local compressors, and to global compres-sors with both relative and absolute compression errors. These gaps motivate this paper. 21.1 Main Contributions 

This paper studies distributed nonconvex optimization with communication compression and addresses the the-oretical gaps identified above by proposing a unified al-gorithm that accommodates both local and global com-pressors. Our contributions are summarized as follows. (i) We simultaneously consider two highly general classes of compressors, the locally-bounded class (Assumption 1) and the globally-bounded class (Assumption 2). In particular, the novel locally-bounded class covers 1-bit compressors and sat-urating quantizers. The globally-bounded class accommodates compressors with both relative and absolute compression errors. It allows commonly used global compressors with only relative or abso-lute errors to tolerate additional arbitrary bounded noise (Lemma 1), and remain within this class un-der bounded-error composition (Lemma 2). Both the locally- and globally-bounded classes are, to the best of our knowledge, the most general in their respective local and global senses, and together they form the most general compressor family in the distributed optimization literature. (ii) We propose a unified compression algorithm (Al-gorithm 1) that applies to both the locally- and globally-bounded compressors. To the best of our knowledge, this is the first distributed optimization algorithm that simultaneously handles local com-pressors, and global compressors with both relative and absolute compression errors. In comparison, Yi et al. (2022) considered local and global compres-sors with only absolute compression error under the P‚ÄìL condition, while our algorithm accommo-dates broader classes of compressors in the noncon-vex setting. This unification exploits the structural parallels between the locally- and globally-bounded compressors, enabling a unified treatment of sev-eral key technical challenges. (iii) For the locally-bounded compressors, we establish convergence in the nonconvex setting and linear convergence under the P‚ÄìL condition. To the best of our knowledge, this is the first convergence guar-antee for general local compressors. In contrast to distributed convex methods with only 1-bit com-pressors or saturating quantizers (Yi and Hong, 2014; Saha et al., 2021), we extend the first analysis to general local compressors and distributed non-convex settings. Notably, the achieved O(1 /‚àöT )convergence rate matches that of centralized algo-rithms with 1-bit compressors (Zhu et al., 2020; Fan et al., 2022). Moreover, with one initial uncom-pressed communication round, the rate improves to O(1 /T 2/3), yielding an order-wise improvement over the existing literature. These results are sum-marized in Theorems 1‚Äì3. (iv) For the globally-bounded compressors, we obtain an O(1 /T ) convergence rate in the nonconvex setting and a linear rate under the P‚ÄìL condi-tion, matching the state-of-the-art guarantees in Liao et al. (2024). Moreover, our method is more communication- and memory-efficient than the method in Liao et al. (2024), since it transmits only one compressed d-dimensional variable per iteration (one less than theirs) and uses three 

d-dimensional auxiliary variables (two less than theirs). These results are summarized in Theo-rems 4 and 5, and Theorems 1‚Äì5 together demon-strate the unification of our algorithm across the locally- and globally-bounded compressors.

In this section, we propose a unified compression algo-rithm that accommodates both the locally- and globally-bounded compressors. Then we highlight the key design that exploits the structural parallels between them, al-lowing a unified treatment of several key technical chal-lenges.

5 Simulations 

Motivated by the edge device application discussed in the introduction, we study distributed training of a long short-term memory language model (Zhuang et al., 2020) to emulate next-word prediction over mo-bile phones. Experiments are conducted on the Penn Treebank dataset using half of the training set, which is evenly split among the n agents representing mobile phones. We set the model dimension to d = 5 ,293 ,200, the truncated backpropagation through time length to 80, and the number of agents to n = 10. Agents com-municate over an ErdÀù os‚ÄìR¬¥ enyi graph with connection probability 0 .4. Performance is evaluated by perplexity (PPL), defined as the exponential of the loss function and computed following Zhuang et al. (2020). Since no existing method handles the locally-bounded compressors in distributed nonconvex settings, whereas RCPP algorithm in Liao et al. (2024) admits the globally-bounded compressors, we compare our algo-rithm with RCPP as well as our uncompressed counter-part, and evaluate the proposed algorithm under various compressors. Specifically, C1 and C2 satisfy Assump-tion 1 with C = 100, and are tested with and without one initial uncompressed communication round. More-over, C3 and C5 satisfy Assumption 2, and are tested in both the noise-free and bounded-noise cases, where the noise bound is set to 100. For remaining compressor parameters, we set ‚àÜ = 1 for C2, k = 1 ,500 ,000 for C3,and k = 4 for C5. In the plots, the above configura-tions are labeled as ‚Äú1-bit‚Äù, ‚Äú1-bit-ini‚Äù, ‚Äúsat‚Äù, ‚Äúsat-ini‚Äù, ‚Äútop-k‚Äù, ‚Äútop-k-n‚Äù, ‚Äú4-bit‚Äù, and ‚Äú4-bit-n‚Äù. For RCPP, we prepend ‚ÄúR-‚Äù to the corresponding label, and our uncompressed counterpart is labeled as ‚Äúuncomp‚Äù. Fig. 2 shows the evolutions of PPL with respect to the number of iterations. The proposed algorithm works well with a variety of compressors. For the locally-bounded compressors, one initial uncompressed com-munication round yields a modest speedup. For the globally-bounded compressors, the proposed algorithm exhibits robustness to the bounded noise. 6 Conclusions 

In this paper, we proposed a unified compression al-gorithm for distributed nonconvex optimization to re-duce communication overhead. The proposed algorithm applies to broader classes of compressors than exist-ing works. Specifically, in the local sense, we provided the convergence for local compressors in the noncon-vex setting, covering low-information compressors such as 1-bit compressors and saturating quantizers. In the global sense, we accommodated global compressors with both relative and absolute compression errors, as well as additional arbitrary bounded noise, thereby covering a broader range of practical compressors. Future work includes time-varying sparse topologies, communication errors, and stochastic-gradient extensions.
