# Abstract 

In this paper, we establish the novel insight that an off-the-shelf LLM can function as an excellent token compres-sor and decompressor. To demonstrate, we design a self-expressive autoencoding learning framework fine-tunes a pretrained LLM to translate long texts into a compact in-ternal language of discrete, variable-length latent codes, termed Z-tokens, and to reconstruct the original text ex-actly from them. The resulting representation is content-adaptive: semantically dense segments receive more Z-tokens, while redundant or predictable regions are ag-gressively compressed, via lightweight LoRA-based adapter heads. Empirically, our method achieves up to 18 × token reduction on Wikipedia, CNN/DailyMail, HotpotQA, and Qulac-style long-query datasets, while preserving recon-struction fidelity and downstream performance. This simple yet effective design supports applications including prompt compression and autoregressive generation directly in the Z-token space, offering a potential pathway toward token-efficient long-context reasoning. 

# 1. Introduction 

Large language models (LLMs) have achieved remarkable success in various fields [4, 34]. However, their ability to handle long contexts remains fundamentally limited by the quadratic complexity of the attention mechanism [36]. As sequence lengths grow to tens or even hundreds of thou-sands of tokens, the computational and memory overhead of the attention mechanism makes large-scale inference, long document understanding, and multi-hop retrieval pro-hibitively expensive. To mitigate this, significant research has focused on to-ken compression, which aims to distill lengthy sequences into shorter, information-dense representations [5, 12, 26, 31]. Existing approaches can be broadly categorized into two categories. Heuristic token merging methods, such as similarity-based merging [2, 45], attention-based prun-

ing [16], and saliency-guided aggregation [20], attempt to remove or merge redundant tokens based on local simi-larity or attention importance scores. While these tech-niques are effective in shortening sequence lengths, to-kens that appear redundant due to similarity may carry dis-course or pragmatic meaning that is difficult to recover. The second category includes learning-based or autoencoding methods such as AutoCompressor [5], ICAE [12], or Gist Tokens [26]. AutoCompressor and ICAE generate fixed-length compressed context vectors that can be re-expanded for downstream tasks by training auxiliary networks or soft embedding modules. Gist Tokens force the model to com-press cues into fixed-length lexical units through an atten-tion masking mechanism. These methods often treat com-pression as an external, auxiliary task, decoupled from the LLM’s primary generative reasoning process. The resulting continuous embeddings or latent codes are non-lexical and lack transparency, making them difficult to interpret or con-trol. Moreover, their reliance on fixed compression ratios prevents adaptation to varying information density and typ-ically forgoes faithful reconstruction of the original input, limiting their applicability in scenarios that demand verifia-bility or fine-grained access to the source content. In this work, we depart from prevailing heuristic and learned-embedding approaches by introducing a novel in-sight: leveraging the semantic knowledge of the LLM to serve as both a token compressor and decompressor. Our method is predicated on the fundamental insight that LLMs, trained on vast corpora, are not merely statistical models but powerful engines for semantic abstraction and concep-tual distillation [3, 9, 37]. We observe that natural lan-guage is inherently compressive; it exhibits a strong ten-dency to encode complex ideas into compact lexical units. Idioms such as “double-edged sword” encapsulate intricate trade-offs, and metaphors like “tip of the iceberg” summa-rize extensive underlying complexities. This phenomenon implies that LLMs itself is capable of efficient compres-sion. Methodologically, we propose an autoencoding train-ing paradigm, where an input sequence X is mapped into a compressed sequence of discrete Z-tokens, Z, by a LLM 1

compressor, and subsequently reconstructed or continued by a LLM decompressor. The training objective can be viewed as a form of bilingual translation, where the model learns to ’encode’ natural language into its own abstract, internal language and ’decode’ it back. The compressed la-tent {Z} serves as a semantic scaffold, forcing the model to perform its core reasoning—such as logic, inference, and narrative planning—on the compact symbolic representa-tion before projecting it back to the surface token level. This mechanism ensures that compression is an act of semantic distillation rather than rote memorization; the model must first understand the input’s meaning to produce an effective Z-token sequence that supports subsequent generation. Our design is more flexible, capable of dynamically lengthen-ing compressed text based on semantic density, and seam-lessly integrating with sliding windows to support the pro-cessing of longer texts. In terms of task design, we support not only text reconstruction and generation but also various downstream tasks such as summarization and long-text QA. We discovered and explained the polysemy of Z-tokens, an interesting phenomenon, and experimentally demonstrated that it is controllable rather than chaotic, providing a novel perspective on text compression. We conducted extensive experiments on six large-scale datasets—Wikipedia, CNN/DailyMail, HotpotQA, Narra-tiveQA, QASPER and QuALITY—for tasks such as text reconstruction, continuation writing, summarization, and long-text question answering. The results show that our method achieves state-of-the-art performance on all tasks, improves inference speed by 4.6 ×, and significantly re-duces memory usage, fully validating the effectiveness and efficiency of the method. 

# 2. Related Work 

Context Compression refers to any mechanism that explic-itly aims to reduce the number of tokens participating in subsequent computation. Some approaches follow heuris-tic token reduction strategies, removing or merging less in-formative tokens based on saliency or similarity. Token Merging [2, 24, 45] aggregates similar tokens , while Token Pruning [16, 19, 29, 32, 45] eliminates low-saliency tokens during inference. Beyond rule-based methods, learning-based semantic compression encodes contexts into compact representations that retain high-level meaning. ICAE [12] and AutoCompress [5] represent long sequences as latent vectors for efficient reconstruction or reasoning; other re-cent works similarly investigate learned context compres-sion [31, 43]. A related branch, prompt compression , targets input instructions rather than the full context. Methods such as 500xCompressor [21], Gist Tokens [26] and LongLLM-Lingua [14] summarize long prompts into shorter, reusable representations. Our method is conceptually aligned with 

learning-based semantic compression but differs in execu-tion: rather than encoding context externally, we integrate compression into the LLM’s reasoning process. This yields discrete internal symbols that form a self-expressive “se-mantic language,” achieving adaptive, interpretable, and se-mantically faithful compression. 

Architectural and Implicit Sparsity Methods represent an alternative line of research that, instead of explic-itly reducing the number of tokens involved in computa-tion, improve efficiency through architectural modifications or implicitly introduced sparsity in the computation pro-cess. Compressive Transformer [28] introduces hierarchi-cal memory buffers summarizing distant activations; Re-former [17] leverages locality-sensitive hashing to approx-imate attention; Routing Transformer [30] sparsifies atten-tion through content-based routing. Other models such as Longformer, BigBird, and Window Attention [1, 6, 7, 10, 11, 25, 39, 41, 42] also achieve scalability via structured sparsity. 

Discrete Semantic Representation provides a principled foundation for compact and interpretable encoding of high-level semantics. VQ-VAE [35] introduced vector-quantized bottlenecks that map continuous signals into a learned code-book, later extended to sequence models [23, 44, 46, 47] and cross-modal learning frameworks [22]. These ap-proaches show that discrete latent variables can serve as symbolic carriers of information. Subsequent works ex-plored training stability and optimization for discrete vari-ables [15]. While prior works used discrete bottlenecks as auxiliary modules, we internalize this semantic layer within the LLM’s reasoning flow, enabling discrete tokens to serve as learned, interpretable units for compressing and reason-ing over long contexts.

This not only brings a huge boost in speed and scalability but also pro-vides a new window into how LLMs internally represent and manipulate semantic knowledge. 43.5. Interpretability Analysis 

We observe that the learned codebook VZ is reused in het-erogeneous inputs: the same Z-tag index can appear in dif-ferent contexts but yields different representations after de-compression; conversely, the same input may have multiple valid Z sequences at different sampling temperatures. Un-like traditional tokenizers, where one token corresponds to one subword, this suggests that Z-tags function as abstract, context-dependent semantic units, rather than deterministic mappings. 

Qualitative evidence. Z-tokens operate at a conceptual level instead of encoding surface wording. 1) Semantic synonymy: sentences such as “The government imple-mented sweeping economic reforms to address the financial crisis” and “Officials enacted major fiscal restructuring to mitigate the debt emergency” share overlapping Z-tokens (e.g., z42 , z 87 ) that capture the idea of government-led eco-nomic intervention, even though the decompressed texts differ lexically. 2) Syntactic alternation: “Climate change threatens agricultural productivity and requires immediate attention” vs. “Agricultural productivity is threatened by climate change, which requires immediate action” map to shared tokens (e.g., z156 , z 203 ) encoding concepts like envi-ronmental threat and urgency, while reconstruction realizes them with different word orders. 3) Domain-level drift: 

“Advanced technology drives innovation in transportation systems” and “Cutting-edge solutions accelerate progress in mobility infrastructure” align on tokens such as z487 , z 99 

that represent technological progress, later re-expanded into context-appropriate phrasings (e.g., modern technology vs. 

state-of-the-art methods ). 

Interpretation. Unlike standard tokenization where sym-bols correspond to fixed subwords, Z-tokens are polyse-mous encodings whose concretization depends on surround-ing codes and decoding context. They achieve higher com-pression by abstracting away surface variability: multiple paraphrases of the same concept are folded onto a shared discrete code, enabling faithful reconstruction of meaning with surface-level diversity.

This ambiguity naturally raises the question: how to transform ambiguity into a deterministic output during de-compression? Z-token decoding is not performed in iso-lation, but rather under three conditions: the complete Z sequence, autoregressive prefixes, and constraints from the base vocabulary. First, we input the complete sequence 

Z = ( z1, . . . , z K ) into the decompressor, narrowing the broad terms down to specific semantic regions through ad-jacent Z-labels. Second, each decoding step is conditioned on the generated text x<t ; once the model generates a spe-cific term, subsequent terms are guided towards that seman-tic direction, rather than irrelevant meanings. Finally, we restrict logits to the pre-trained vocabulary Vbase , leverag-ing the power of LLM to ensure that abstract terms appear as frequently occurring and well-trained natural language terms, rather than arbitrary substitutions. These three con-straints transform ambiguity into a context-dependent im-plementation, just as words in natural language may be am-biguous, but sentences are usually clear within their context.

4.3. Metrics 

For text reconstruction and continuation tasks, we use BLEU-4 as the core evaluation metric. Additionally, we report the perplexity score (PPL), which quantifies the dif-ficulty of the model predicting the text. For the QuAL-ITY dataset, we report the exact match (EM) score. For QuALITY, NarrativeQA, and QASPER, we report the F1 score. For the CNN/DailyMail summarization task, we pro-vide ROUGE-1, ROUGE-2, and ROUGE-L scores. To re-duce bias from randomness and ensure the reliability of the results, all experiments were independently repeated three times, and the average score for each experiment is re-ported.

4.5. Ablation Study. 

We first explore the impact of schedule-sampling on free-decoding performance in a Wikipedia reconstruction task. We use ICAE as the baseline, which relies on Teacher-Forcing during decoding. This approach leads to exposure bias; the model performs well during training, but its per-formance rapidly declines during free decoding due to the autoregressive accumulation of errors. To mitigate this is-sue, we introduce schedule-sampling during training 3.2, al-lowing the model to learn to address this bias during train-ing. It’s important to note that our goal is not to com-pletely eliminate exposure bias, but rather to verify whether the model’s robustness to free decoding can be effectively learned. Therefore, we only set the sampling probability to gradually increase from 0 to 0.5. While p → 1 further improves the stability of free decoding, it also slows down the convergence speed and may negatively impact language fluency. Under this controlled setting, the results in Ta-ble 6 show that Schedule-Sampling effectively improves the quality of free decoding reconstruction and is beneficial for real-world reasoning scenarios. To assess the impact of the number of Z-tokens, we con-duct experiments on HotpotQA and evaluate settings of 1k, 2k, 4k, 8k, and 10k. The results are shown in Table 8. When the number of Z-tokens is too small, the compressed representation becomes overly compact and fails to retain sufficient relational and contextual information, resulting in poorer QA performance. As the number of Z-tokens in-creases, more semantic structure is preserved and perfor-mance improves accordingly. However, beyond a certain point (e.g., from 8k to 10k), the performance gain becomes marginal, indicating that the representation has reached a saturation region where key reasoning information is al-ready captured. This reveals a balance between compres-sion rate and performance, where adequate semantic cov-erage is achieved without incurring unnecessary computa-tional overhead.

4.6. Conclusion. 

In this paper, we propose a discretization method that in-tegrates compression and generation into the same LLM, allowing the model to naturally compress long texts into variable-length, controllable, and interpretable Z-tokens be-fore executing downstream tasks, achieving a maximum inference speedup of 3.4× and significant memory sav-ings. Extensive experiments across multiple tasks have 8demonstrated the effectiveness of this ”LLM speaks its own short language” approach, providing a novel perspec-tive on text compression. Future work could focus on two key areas: first, designing a global Z-token coordina-tion mechanism for long-range dependencies across win-dows; and second, making the compression process task-aware, preserving factual or argumentative structures as needed.
